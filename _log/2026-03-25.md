## Notes
### Literature
1. Prioritization is *really* powerful. According to this paper, prioritization was found to be the most important ingredient in an ablation study carried out in an ablation study where multiple techniques in reinforcement learning were combined (future reference: in the Rainbow model).
2. Alright, the architecture seems quite straightforward: the agents feed the replay buffer, which in turn is used to train. Got it!
3. It's nice to see that this trend of decomposing the agent into an actor and learner is, at this point in the literature, becoming widespread. I think it makes a ton of sense myself, although I can see why it would have taken a bit of time for this to catch on - this is almost exclusively a hardware driven change.
4. In distributed PER, the actors contribute to a centralized replay buffer. There is also a centralized learner that shares parameters. The method used here (multistep DQN and DPG) is off-policy anyway, so sampling with respect to policy lag isn't really anything to worry about. The real key, of course, is the priorisation that takes place within the learner.
5. Oof. Haven't come across duelling architectures yet - added to my literature review list.
6. There's something interesting about Figure 3, where the mean episode return is plotted for a set of actors. For the humanoid (stand) game, the episode return suddenly falls to zero for a period before rising back up. What caused that?
7. **Limitation of these approaches**: They can only really work in situations where data is easy to obtain.
