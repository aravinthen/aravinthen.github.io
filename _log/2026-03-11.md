## Summary
* More progress on *Effective Python* by Slatkin.

## Notes
1. You know, I never really understood setter/getter classes when I first came across them. I now realise that this was all a consequence of Python being my first language. Still, it's nice to have the `@property` decorator in mind - there have been a ton of situations where, say, I had to compute an attribute and using `@property` would make sense.
2. Ah, hold up. It looks like `@property` is way more powerful than I assumed. They're essentially means of intercepting the attribute as it's being set, but in a much cleaner way. *Of course*, in a language where getter/setters are used routinely, you'd have the ability to employ interception in the getter/setters, but in Python we
    * want to support attribute exposure,
    * still want the ability to modify/validate/change an attribute as it's being set.
3. This is making me realise that object-oriented programming isn't just 'hur-dur-objects-interacting', it's very much focused on the precise engineering of *machines* that process information.
